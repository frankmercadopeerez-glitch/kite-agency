When is the Best Time to Kitesurf in Cartagena?

By Frank Mercado • 8 Min Read

Cartagena de Indias is a Caribbean jewel, famous for its colonial walls and vibrant culture. But for us water sports enthusiasts, it is one of the most reliable wind machines in South America. If you are planning a trip to Colombia and want to score the best sessions, timing is everything.

As a naval consultant and kitesurf instructor, I have analyzed the local weather patterns for years. Here is the definitive guide to scoring wind in Cartagena.

1. The Golden Season: "Los Alisios" (December - April)

This is the prime time. The Northeast Trade Winds (Alisios) blow with incredible consistency.

  • Wind Speed: 18 to 28 knots.
  • Consistency: 90% of days.
  • Direction: Side-onshore (NNE).
  • Best Kite Size: 9m or 10m for an average rider (75kg).

Pro Tip: The wind usually picks up around 11:00 AM and stays strong until sunset (6:00 PM). This allows for perfect "sleep-in" mornings or cultural tours before heading to the beach.

2. The "Veranillo de San Juan" (July - August)

Many people miss this window. We get a mini-season in the middle of the year. The winds can actually be stronger than in the main season (sometimes hitting 30 knots), but they might be slightly more gusty. It is less crowded than December, making it a great option for serious riders who want the ocean to themselves.

3. What Gear Should I Bring?

If you are bringing your own quiver:

  • Kites: A 9m and a 12m will cover 95% of days. If you are light, bring a 7m.
  • Board: Standard twin-tip. The chop can be active, so a board with good rocker helps.
  • Wetsuit? Not needed. The water is warm (27°C / 80°F). A rashguard for sun protection is essential.

Don't want to travel with oversized luggage? At Marine Tech, we rent the latest Duotone and Cabrinha gear tailored to the day's conditions.

4. The Spots: La Boquilla vs. Manzanillo

La Boquilla: This is where we operate. It has wide sandy beaches, no rocks, and fewer swimmers than the city center (Bocagrande). It is the safest place to learn.

Manzanillo del Mar: Located further north, it offers wider beaches and is slightly more secluded. We organize downwind trips starting here.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe for beginners?

Yes. The side-onshore wind direction means you always drift back to the beach, never out to sea. Plus, our instructors use boat support.

What if there is no wind?

We offer Foil Tow-in lessons behind a boat, or Stand Up Paddle (SUP) tours in the mangroves.

How many hours to learn?

Most students need between 8 to 12 hours to become independent (IKO Level 3). Check our "Rider" course.
