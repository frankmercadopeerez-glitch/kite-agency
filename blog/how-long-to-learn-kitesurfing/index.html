How Long Does It Take to Learn to Kitesurf? The Honest Answer

By Frank Mercado • April 1, 2026 • 7 Min Read

"I only have 5 days in Cartagena. Will I be riding independently by the end of the trip?" This is the question I hear most often — and it deserves a completely honest answer, not a marketing pitch. So here it is, broken down phase by phase.

The Three Phases of Learning

The path to riding independently can be divided into three clear phases. Each one builds on the last, and skipping any phase creates dangerous gaps in skill.

Phase 1: Kite Control (2–4 Hours)

This is all on land (or in shallow water with just the kite). You learn to fly the kite through the "wind window" — the invisible 3D space in front of you where the kite can be flown. You practice power zones, emergency releases, and re-launching from the water. This phase feels slow but is the most critical. A person with good kite control can learn to ride; a person without it is a danger to themselves and others on the beach.

Goal of Phase 1: Fly the kite with one hand, eyes closed, in any position of the wind window, and activate the safety instantly.

Phase 2: Body Dragging (2–4 Hours)

Now you enter the water — without the board. The kite pulls your body across the surface. You learn to steer left, steer right, go upwind, go downwind, and most importantly: recover your board when you lose it (which will happen a lot). Body dragging is also where your kite skills truly become automatic. When your brain isn't worried about staying on the board, it can focus purely on the kite.

Goal of Phase 2: Body drag upwind to retrieve a lost board without losing kite control.

Phase 3: Water Start & Riding (4–8 Hours)

This is the phase everyone wants to jump to. Board on feet, kite powered, you dive the kite and let it pull you up onto the water's surface. The first attempts are humbling — you'll crash, get pulled forward, get pulled backward, and swallow some water. This is completely normal and part of the process. Most students get their first few meters of riding somewhere in this phase.

Goal of Phase 3: Ride 50+ meters consistently in one direction without falling.

The Honest Timeline

Here's a realistic breakdown based on our experience teaching hundreds of students:

Type of Student Hours to First Ride Hours to Independence
No board sport experience, follows instructions well 8–10 hours 12–15 hours
Surfer, skater, or kiteboarding experience 5–7 hours 8–12 hours
Previous kite experience (body dragging done) 3–5 hours 6–8 hours

So yes — 5 days in Cartagena is absolutely enough for most students to reach independence, especially if you do 2–3 hours per day (which is the ideal for retaining information).

What Actually Slows You Down

  • Rushing Phase 1: Students who skip proper kite control training spend 3× longer in Phase 3 crashing repeatedly.
  • Fatigue: 4+ hour sessions lead to mental tiredness, poor decision-making, and slower motor learning. 2 hours/day is scientifically better than one 6-hour marathon session.
  • Bad conditions: Learning in 30-knot gusty wind is harder than in steady 18 knots. In Cartagena, we always assess conditions before committing to a session — your investment is protected.
  • The wrong school: An instructor who puts you on a board too early causes frustration and bad habits that take hours to unlearn.

The Rider Course (8H) is specifically calibrated to take a complete beginner to independence — not in theory, but based on real results with real students in Cartagena's specific conditions.
